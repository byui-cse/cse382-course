Helps:

All prime numbers are of the form \(6n \pm 1\), but not all numbers of the form \(6n \pm 1\) are prime. An example of a \(6n \pm 1\) non-prime would be 25 (\(6\cdot 4+1\)). However for \(n\in\mathbb{Z}^+\)if \(n\) is prime, \((n-1)!\equiv -1\pmod n\) (Wilson's Theorem). While these truths are not computationally efficient, they are sufficient for the limited cases in the unit tests. Use these two truths about primes in your implementation of the generate_primes function.
